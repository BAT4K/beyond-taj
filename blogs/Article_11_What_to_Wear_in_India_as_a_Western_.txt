# What to Wear in India as a Western Woman — The Practical Guide

**Meta description:** What to actually pack, what to wear at temples, on trains, in cities, and in rural areas. Specific items, specific situations, no vague advice.

**Target keyword:** what to wear india western woman

---

This isn't a fashion article. It's a practical guide to what works on the ground — for comfort, safety, cultural respect, and not getting turned away at temple entrances.

## The Core Rule

**Cover shoulders and knees in any setting outside of beach resorts and major city malls.** This applies to temples, rural areas, small towns, trains, and most public spaces. In Goa, Mumbai nightlife, and resort pools — Western clothing is fine.

## The Wardrobe That Works Everywhere

**Buy kurtas in India.** A knee-length kurta (loose tunic) over leggings or loose cotton pants is the single most versatile outfit for a woman in India. It works at temples, on trains, in markets, in restaurants, and in rural areas. You can buy beautiful ones in India for ₹300-800 ($4-10). No need to pack them from home.

**Specific packing list:**
- 2-3 loose cotton/linen pants (not yoga pants, not leggings alone)
- 2-3 kurtas or long loose shirts that cover your hips
- 1 long cotton skirt (ankle-length, works for all temples)
- 1 large cotton scarf/dupatta (head covering for temples, mosques, gurdwaras)
- 1 light cardigan or long-sleeve layer (for AC trains and religious sites)
- Comfortable closed-toe shoes or sandals with back straps (you remove shoes constantly — slip-ons save time)
- A sarong (emergency cover-up, beach towel, privacy screen on sleeper trains)

## Temple Dress Codes — Region by Region

**North India (Delhi, Rajasthan, Varanasi, Amritsar):** Cover shoulders and knees. Jeans with a long kurta are usually fine. Golden Temple requires head covering (scarves provided free at the entrance).

**South India (Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka):** Stricter. Many temples require traditional Indian attire — salwar kameez or long skirt with covered shoulders. Jeans may be refused at temples like Guruvayur (Kerala), Meenakshi (Madurai), or Tirupati. A long cotton skirt + covered top is the safest choice.

**Mosques:** Cover arms, legs, and hair. Shoes off. Loose clothing.

**Sikh Gurdwaras:** Cover head (scarves provided). Modest clothing. Shoes off.

**No dress code at the Taj Mahal** — this surprises people, but it's true.

## What NOT to Wear

- **Shorts in public** (outside of beach/resort areas) — draws stares and can restrict temple entry
- **Tank tops or spaghetti straps** — uncomfortable level of attention in most of India
- **Tight leggings without a long top** — culturally reads differently in India than in the West
- **Anything transparent or low-cut** — not about judgment, about practical reality
- **Expensive jewelry in crowded areas** — theft risk

## Situation-Specific Advice

**On overnight trains:** Loose cotton pants + long t-shirt. Bring a light blanket (Indian Railways provides one but it's thin). The sarong doubles as a privacy curtain on side-lower berths.

**In Rajasthan/desert areas:** Long sleeves protect from sun better than sunscreen. Light cotton, light colors. A scarf protects your hair and face from dust.

**In Kerala/humid areas:** Cotton or linen only. Synthetic fabrics in Kerala humidity are miserable. Quick-dry travel clothes work well.

**In Himalayan areas (Ladakh, Himachal, Sikkim):** Layer system — thermal base, fleece mid, windproof outer. Temperatures drop sharply after sunset even in summer.

## The Kurta Strategy

Many experienced female travelers in India adopt a "kurta wardrobe" by day three. Here's why:

- Instantly reduces unwanted attention (you blend in more)
- Gets warmer reception from locals (seen as culturally respectful)
- Comfortable in heat
- Temple-ready at all times
- Cheap to buy locally and leave behind when you fly home

Best places to buy: Fabindia (mid-range, all major cities), local markets (₹300-600), Janpath Market Delhi, Bapu Bazaar Jaipur.

## Takeaway

Pack light, buy kurtas locally, carry one long skirt for South Indian temples, and always have a scarf. The clothing choices that reduce attention and increase comfort are also the ones that show cultural respect.
