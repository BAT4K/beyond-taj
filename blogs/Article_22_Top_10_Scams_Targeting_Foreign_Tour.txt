# Top 10 Scams Targeting Foreign Tourists in India (And How to Avoid Every One)

**Meta description:** The 10 most common India tourist scams explained step by step — how they start, how they work, and the exact response that shuts each one down.

**Target keyword:** common scams india foreigners

---

These scams follow the same script every time. Once you know the pattern, they're easy to spot and shut down.

## 1. The "Hotel Is Closed" Scam
**Where:** Delhi airport, train stations
**Script:** Taxi driver says your booked hotel is "closed," "flooded," or "moved." He knows a "better one."
**Reality:** Your hotel is fine. He earns commission from the replacement.
**Fix:** Call your hotel directly. Never change plans based on a driver's claim.

## 2. The Gem/Carpet Investment Scam
**Where:** Delhi, Jaipur, Agra
**Script:** Friendly local → chai → "my brother's export shop" → you're shown "government-approved" gems/carpets → told you can resell at home for profit.
**Reality:** Goods are worth 10-20% of what you pay. No resale market exists.
**Fix:** Never follow a stranger to a shop. Period.

## 3. The Free Rickshaw Tour
**Where:** Delhi, Varanasi, Jaipur
**Script:** Auto driver offers a free or ₹10 city tour → takes you to 4-5 commission shops → each stop lasts 20-30 min.
**Reality:** Driver earns 30-40% commission. Your "free tour" costs hours and pressure.
**Fix:** Refuse any tour that sounds too cheap. Hire a licensed guide instead.

## 4. The Fake Tourist Office
**Where:** Near New Delhi Railway Station, Connaught Place
**Script:** Official-looking office with "Government Approved" signs → inflated tour packages → non-existent hotels.
**Reality:** India's official tourist office is at 88 Janpath, New Delhi. Everything near the train station is fake.
**Fix:** Book only through your hotel, a verified online platform, or the official India Tourism office at 88 Janpath.

## 5. The Overcharged Rickshaw
**Where:** Everywhere
**Script:** Driver quotes 3-5x the real fare.
**Fix:** Use Uber/Ola Auto. Or say "meter chalu karo" (start the meter) and walk away if refused.

## 6. The Blessing Scam
**Where:** Varanasi, Pushkar, temple areas
**Script:** A "priest" ties a thread on your wrist or applies a tikka → demands ₹500-2,000 as "donation."
**Fix:** Politely decline any unsolicited blessing. If they've already applied it, give ₹20-50 and walk away. You owe nothing for something you didn't ask for.

## 7. The Wrong Change
**Where:** Markets, street vendors
**Script:** Vendor gives change with old/damaged notes or short-changes by palming a note.
**Fix:** Count change before walking away. Learn to recognize Indian currency denominations. Carry small bills.

## 8. The SIM Card Markup
**Where:** Airports, tourist areas
**Script:** Unofficial kiosk charges ₹2,000-3,000 for a SIM worth ₹500-700.
**Fix:** Buy from official Jio or Airtel counters inside airport terminals. Bring passport + photo.

## 9. The "Closed Today" Monument Redirect
**Where:** Agra, Delhi, Jaipur
**Script:** Someone near a monument tells you "it's closed today" or "there's a special ceremony" → redirects you to a shop.
**Reality:** The monument is open. Check Google or call your hotel.
**Fix:** Ignore unsolicited information about closures. Walk to the ticket counter yourself.

## 10. The Taxi Meter "Broken"
**Where:** Airports, train stations
**Script:** Driver says meter is broken → quotes flat rate 3-5x the real cost.
**Fix:** Use prepaid taxi counters inside terminals. Or use Uber/Ola.

## The One Universal Rule

**If someone approaches you unsolicited at a tourist site with helpful information, directions, or an offer — they are almost certainly working.** Genuine helpful Indians exist (many of them), but they don't hang around tourist sites looking for foreigners to help.

## Takeaway

Every scam on this list follows a predictable pattern. Know the 10 scripts, use apps for transport, and never follow a stranger to a shop. That covers 95% of the risk.
