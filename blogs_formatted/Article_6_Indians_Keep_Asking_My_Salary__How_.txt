# Indians Keep Asking My Salary — How to Handle It

**Meta description:** The salary question in India is cultural, not rude. Here are 3 responses that work without offending anyone, and why the question is being asked.

**Target keyword:** indians asking my salary

---

It will happen in the first week. A taxi driver, a hotel staffer, a new acquaintance at dinner — someone will ask what you earn. Maybe how much your phone cost. Maybe how much your flight was. You'll freeze. Here's what's actually happening and how to respond.

## Why They're Asking

In much of India, discussing income, costs, and personal finances is a normal social behavior — not rude. It serves several functions:

- **Social placement** — understanding where someone fits in the economic landscape
- **Conversational currency** — the way some cultures discuss weather, Indians discuss costs
- **Genuine curiosity** — especially in rural or smaller-town India, foreign incomes are abstract and interesting
- **Comparison without malice** — "you earn more, we earn less, let's be honest about it"

**The question is not asked to embarrass you.** It's asked because that's how conversation works in many Indian social contexts. Responding with offense creates confusion, not resolution.

## Three Responses That Work

**1. Deflect with humor**
"Enough to be here, but not enough to do it twice!" — Gets a laugh. Ends the line. Works every time.

**2. Give a vague but honest range**
"Enough for rent and travel — not much left after that." — Sounds honest without providing a number. Satisfies the curiosity.

**3. Redirect**
"In our country we don't usually discuss salary — but tell me about your work!" — Turns the conversation around. Gives them what they actually wanted, which was connection.

## What NOT to Do

- **Don't lie with a specific number.** If you say ₹30,000/month to seem relatable, any Indian who knows Western costs will see through it instantly.
- **Don't respond with offense.** "That's rude in my country" shuts down a potential friendship and confuses the person.
- **Don't lecture about privacy norms.** The cultural gap is real but explaining it mid-conversation is awkward for both sides.

## When It's a Setup (10% of the Time)

If the salary question comes from someone trying to sell you something — a shop owner, a tout, a "friendly" stranger near a tourist site — the question is calibrating your budget for a sales pitch. The tell: it's immediately followed by "come see my brother's shop" or "I know a special place."

In this case, any response is fine because the conversation itself is the setup. The move is to end the conversation: "No thank you, we have plans" — and keep walking.

## Takeaway

The salary question is cultural, not hostile. Have a deflection ready, use it warmly, and redirect to a real conversation. Most of the time, the person asking just wants to connect. Let them.
