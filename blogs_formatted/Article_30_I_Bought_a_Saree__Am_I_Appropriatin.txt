# I Bought a Saree — Am I Appropriating Indian Culture?

**Meta description:** The honest answer from Indian women — when wearing a saree is honoring culture and when it isn't, with specific scenarios and real perspectives.

**Target keyword:** is wearing a saree cultural appropriation

---

If you bought a saree in India and now you're wondering whether to wear it at home, here's what Indian women actually say.

## The Short Answer

The overwhelming majority of Indian women welcome foreigners wearing Indian clothing — when worn with respect and context. India's relationship with cultural sharing is generally open and celebratory, not gatekeeping.

## When It's Honoring

- Wearing a saree at an Indian wedding you're invited to
- Wearing a kurta or salwar kameez while traveling in India (this is actively appreciated)
- Wearing Indian clothing to a cultural event (Diwali, Navratri)
- Buying from local artisans and understanding the craft

## When It Gets Uncomfortable

- Wearing religious symbols (Om, sacred geometry) as fashion accessories
- Posting "spiritual India" content that performs your enlightenment
- Wearing a bindi as a music festival accessory (contested — some Indians don't care, many find it reductive)
- Using Indian dress in a costume or party context

## The Real Framework

> "Am I wearing this with the same care an Indian woman would?"

If yes — you're honoring. If no — you're costuming. The line is about respect and context, not about permission.

## The Bindi Specifically

This is where opinions split among Indian women:
- Many don't care at all and find the Western anxiety performative
- Some find it uncomfortable when a bindi is worn as generic "ethnic" fashion with no understanding of its significance
- Almost everyone agrees: wearing a bindi at an Indian event while in India is completely fine

## Takeaway

Wear the saree. Wear the kurta. Do it with respect, context, and without performing someone else's spirituality for Instagram. Most Indians will be delighted, not offended.

**BeyondTaj travelers who attend Indian weddings or festivals during their trip are typically dressed by their host family — the saree becomes a relationship, not a souvenir.**
