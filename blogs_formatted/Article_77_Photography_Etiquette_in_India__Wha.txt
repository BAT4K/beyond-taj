# Photography Etiquette in India — What You Can and Can't Photograph

**Meta description:** When to ask permission, when to put the camera away, the selfie request phenomenon, and the specific places where photography is prohibited.

**Target keyword:** photography etiquette india tourists

---

India is one of the most photogenic countries on earth. It's also a place where pointing a camera at the wrong thing creates real problems. Here's the etiquette.

## Always Ask Permission

**Before photographing people:** A simple gesture toward your camera + raised eyebrows is universally understood. Most people say yes enthusiastically. Some say no. Respect the no.

**Especially important:**
- Women (photographing women without consent is considered harassment)
- Children (parents' permission required)
- People bathing at ghats (this is private ritual, not a photo opportunity)
- Religious practitioners in prayer
- Sadhus (holy men) — many expect a tip (₹20-50) for photos. Some refuse entirely.

## Never Photograph

- **Cremations at burning ghats** (Varanasi, Haridwar) — deeply disrespectful. You will be confronted.
- **Military installations, bridges, airports** — illegal and enforced
- **Some government buildings** — look for "no photography" signs
- **Inside the inner sanctum of most temples** — usually prohibited. Outer courtyards are usually fine.
- **Courts and police stations** — prohibited

## The Selfie Request Phenomenon

You will be asked for selfies. Sometimes 5-10 times per hour at major tourist sites.

**When to say yes:** When it's families, students, or curious locals who genuinely want a photo with a foreigner. It's flattering and harmless.

**When to say no:** When you're exhausted, when the crowd is growing, when the request feels aggressive, when men are getting too close (solo women especially). "No photo please" with a smile and a wave is enough.

**Tip:** If you agree to one selfie at a crowded monument, be prepared for 10 more immediately. The first yes creates a queue. If you're short on time, decline from the start.

## Best Practices

- **Shoot in the morning.** 6-8 a.m. has the best light and fewest crowds.
- **Dress down.** A massive DSLR draws more attention (and more selfie requests) than a phone or compact camera.
- **Give back.** If you photograph someone, show them the photo. Offer to send it via WhatsApp. This transforms a transaction into a connection.
- **Buy something.** If you photograph a vendor, a shop, or a market stall — buy from them. It's courtesy.
- **Don't photograph poverty for Instagram.** Photographing homeless people, beggars, or slums without consent and context is exploitative. If you visit Dharavi, go with an ethical tour that involves the community.

## Drone Rules

Drones require a license in India (DGCA registration). Flying an unlicensed drone is illegal and can result in confiscation and fines. Most tourist sites explicitly prohibit drones. Leave it at home unless you've done the legal paperwork.

## Takeaway

Ask before shooting people. Never shoot cremations. Say no to selfie requests when you need to. Morning light is the best. Give back to the people you photograph.
