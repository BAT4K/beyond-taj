# Why Do Indians Want Photos With Foreigners?

**Meta description:** The selfie requests are curiosity, not creepiness. Here's why it happens, how to handle it, and when to say no.

**Target keyword:** why do indians want photos foreigners

---

If you're asked for 10 selfies per hour at a tourist site, here's what's actually happening.

## Why

- **Genuine novelty.** In much of India outside major cities, foreign faces are uncommon. You're interesting the way a celebrity would be in your hometown.
- **Social media culture.** India has 600+ million smartphone users. A photo with a foreigner gets likes and comments. You're content.
- **Family bragging rights.** Showing relatives "I met a foreigner at the Taj" is a social currency.
- **No cultural taboo against asking.** In Western culture, photographing strangers is awkward. In India, it's friendly.

## How to Handle It

**When to say yes:** Families, students, children with parents present. It's 15 seconds, it makes their day, and you get a genuine smile in return.

**When to say no:** When you're exhausted, when the crowd is growing (one yes = ten more), when men are getting too close (solo women especially), or when it just doesn't feel right.

**The script:** "No photo please" + smile + wave + keep walking. No explanation needed.

**The crowd management trick:** If you say yes once at a busy monument, expect a queue to form. If you need to move on, say "last one!" after 3-4 photos and walk away decisively.

## Is It Ever Creepy?

Rarely. The vast majority of requests are genuine curiosity. Red flags: photos taken without asking, a man insisting on putting his arm around a solo woman, or someone who won't accept "no." If any of these happen, walk toward other tourists or security.

## Takeaway

The selfie phenomenon is curiosity and social media culture, not creepiness. Say yes when comfortable, no when not. It fades after your first few days as it becomes routine.
