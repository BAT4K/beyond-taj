# Top 10 Scams Targeting Foreign Tourists in India

**Meta description:** Avoid the "fake train ticket office," the "your hotel burnt down" scam, and the "gem smuggling" trap. Here is our definitive guide to the top 10 tourist scams in India and exactly how to outsmart them.

**Target keyword:** scams in india foreign tourists

---

India is an incredibly hospitable country, but its major tourist hubs (Delhi, Agra, Jaipur, and Varanasi) are infamous for highly organized scams targeting first-time foreign visitors.

The good news? These scams are entirely predictable. They follow the exact same scripts they have followed for decades. If you know the script, you are virtually immune. 

Here are the top 10 scams you will encounter, and exactly how to avoid them.

## 1. The "Your Hotel Burnt Down / Is Closed" Scam
* **Where it happens:** Delhi (especially outside the airport or New Delhi Railway Station).
* **The Script:** You get into a taxi or auto-rickshaw. The driver pretends to call your hotel (or drives you to a fake "Tourist Information" office) and tells you that your hotel has burned down, is closed due to a festival, or the road is blocked by a riot. They will then offer to take you to a "safe" hotel (where they get a massive commission) or force you to book an overpriced tour.
* **How to beat it:** Simply smile and say, "That's okay, take me to the exact address anyway. My friend is waiting outside." Never hand your phone to the driver. If they refuse to move, get out and book an Uber.

## 2. The Fake "Official" Tourist Office
* **Where it happens:** Delhi (Connaught Place, Paharganj).
* **The Script:** Touts will notice you looking at a map and say, "The train station booking office has moved. You must go to the Official Government Tourist Office." They lead you to a private travel agency with fake government signage (often claiming to be DTTDC or Incredible India). There, they will tell you all trains are sold out and scam you into buying a $500 private taxi tour.
* **How to beat it:** Ignore anyone who approaches you on the street offering directions to a ticket office. Book trains online via 12Go Asia or the official IRCTC app. 

## 3. The "Fast Track" Temple Guide
* **Where it happens:** Varanasi Ghats, Pushkar Lake, major temples.
* **The Script:** A friendly local approaches you, offers to explain the history of the temple, and blesses you with a string bracelet or flower petal. After a 5-minute chat, they demand an extortionate "donation" (often $20-$50 USD) for the priest, their family, or karma.
* **How to beat it:** A polite but firm "No thank you" while keeping your hands in your pockets. If you want a guide, hire one officially through your hotel. 

## 4. The Gem Smuggling Trap
* **Where it happens:** Jaipur, Agra, Goa.
* **The Script:** A charismatic shop owner befriends you over chai. They propose a "foolproof" business deal: you carry high-value gems back to your home country to bypass export taxes, and their "partner" will meet you at the airport and pay you a massive commission. They ask you to leave a "small deposit" on your credit card for collateral.
* **How to beat it:** The gems are worthless colored glass. You will never see your deposit again. Never agree to transport goods across international borders for strangers.

## 5. The Phony Train Ticket Examiner (TTE)
* **Where it happens:** Major railway stations.
* **The Script:** A man wearing a slightly official-looking badge stops you on the platform. He asks to see your ticket, frowns, and tells you it is invalid, cancelled, or you need to pay a "foreigner quota fine" in cash right now or you cannot board.
* **How to beat it:** The real TTE (Ticket Collector) wears a black coat and a name badge, and will only check your ticket *after* you are seated on the train, not on the platform. Ignore anyone demanding money on the platform.

## The Verdict

Scams in India rely entirely on a foreigner's confusion, politeness, and fear of causing a scene. 

**The golden rule of traveling in India:** You do not have to be polite to people who approach you on the street aggressively selling a service. A firm "No" and continuing to walk is the safest strategy.
