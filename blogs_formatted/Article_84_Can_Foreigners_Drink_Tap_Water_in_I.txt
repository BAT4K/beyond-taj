# Can Foreigners Drink Tap Water in India?

**Meta description:** No. Here's why, what to drink instead, the ice question, and how to stay hydrated safely.

**Target keyword:** can foreigners drink tap water india

---

**No.** Do not drink tap water anywhere in India. This applies to all cities, all hotels, all restaurants.

## Why

Indian tap water contains bacteria, parasites, and in some areas heavy metals that foreign digestive systems are not adapted to. Even Indians in different regions get sick from local water when they travel domestically.

## What to Drink

- **Sealed bottled water.** Check the seal — squeeze the cap to confirm it clicks. Some vendors refill bottles with tap water.
- **Boiled water.** Hotels that serve water in jugs have typically boiled and filtered it. Ask to confirm.
- **Filtered water.** Many hotels and restaurants use RO (reverse osmosis) filters. "RO water" or "filtered water" is safe.

## The Ice Question

- **Restaurant ice (clear, uniform cubes):** Machine-made from purified water. Safe in quality restaurants.
- **Street vendor ice (cloudy, irregular chunks):** Made from tap water. Avoid.
- **Rule of thumb:** If the restaurant looks clean and established, the ice is fine. If you're at a roadside stall, skip ice.

## Brushing Teeth

Use bottled water for brushing teeth during your first week. After that, most travelers switch to tap water for brushing without issues — the risk is primarily from swallowing significant amounts.

## Staying Hydrated

India's heat dehydrates faster than you expect. Drink 3-4 liters per day (more in Rajasthan summer). Carry ORS sachets (Electral, ₹20) for heavy sweating days. Signs of dehydration: dark urine, headache, dizziness.

## The Budget Hack

A reusable water bottle with a built-in filter (LifeStraw, Grayl) saves ₹40-60/day on bottled water and reduces plastic waste. Worth the ₹2,000-4,000 investment.

## Takeaway

Never tap water. Always sealed bottles, filtered, or boiled. Check the seal. Skip cloudy ice. Carry ORS. Hydrate aggressively.
