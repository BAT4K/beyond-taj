# I'm LGBTQ+ — Should I Go to India?

**Meta description:** Same-sex relationships are legal in India since 2018. Here's what that means on the ground — city by city, hotel by hotel, and what to actually expect.

**Target keyword:** lgbtq travel india safe

---

The legal answer is clear. The lived experience is more textured. Here's what LGBTQ+ travelers actually need to know.

## The Legal Situation

India's Supreme Court struck down Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code in September 2018, decriminalizing consensual same-sex relationships. This is settled law.

**What hasn't changed:**
- Same-sex marriage is not legally recognized
- No legal recognition of same-sex partnerships
- No adoption rights for same-sex couples
- A 2023 Supreme Court petition for marriage equality was declined

The legal environment is: you will not be arrested, but you also won't be legally recognized as a couple.

## What It's Actually Like on the Ground

**Public displays of affection:** Even straight Indian couples avoid PDA in public — this is cultural, not specifically anti-LGBTQ. Two men or two women holding hands actually reads as normal in India (male friends routinely hold hands). Kissing in public is unusual for anyone.

**Hotels — sharing a room:**
- International chains (Marriott, Taj, Oberoi, ITC, Hyatt): No issues. Ever.
- Heritage hotels and boutique properties: Rarely an issue, especially if booked under both names.
- Budget guesthouses in conservative areas: Occasionally awkward. Two men sharing a room raises no flags. Two women may get a confused look in very small towns but are almost never refused.

**Visibly LGBTQ-friendly cities:**
- **Mumbai** — India's most tolerant city for LGBTQ+ expression. Visible queer scene, pride events, LGBTQ-friendly bars (especially Bandra, Colaba)
- **Bangalore** — Strong tech-sector progressive culture
- **Goa** — Relaxed beach culture, tolerant
- **Pondicherry** — French-influenced, cosmopolitan for its size
- **Delhi (South Delhi)** — Progressive neighborhoods, LGBTQ events
- **Fort Kochi, Kerala** — Tolerant, multicultural tourist economy

**Cities where same-sex travelers should be more discreet:**
- Smaller towns in UP, MP, Rajasthan (outside heritage hotels)
- Very conservative rural areas
- Religious pilgrimage sites during peak festivals

## Practical Advice

- Book international or known-friendly properties
- In booking forms, use both names — this prevents front-desk confusion at check-in
- India's LGBTQ+ community is active and welcoming — Pink Pages India and Gaysifamily.com are good resources for local events and community
- For transgender travelers: India has a long-recognized hijra (third-gender) tradition. Western trans identities are read differently but not hostilely in tourist contexts. Passport gender will be accepted at face value in hotels and airports.

## Takeaway

India is legally safe for LGBTQ+ travelers since 2018. The social experience varies by city — Mumbai, Bangalore, Goa, and Delhi are openly tolerant. Smaller towns require more discretion. Hotel sharing is a non-issue at quality properties.
